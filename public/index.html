Climate Action

COP21 Lights the Path

At COP21, also known as the 2015 Paris Climate Conference, 195 nations and the European Union signed on to reduce greenhouse gas emissions1 with the goal of keeping global warming below 2°C.

What the U.S. is doing

The U.S. is planning to cut greenhouse gas emissions from major sources.2 Hear about the Historic Paris Agreement from EPA Administrator Gina McCarthy.

Power Plants

Under the Clean Air Act, EPA's Clean Power Plan cuts carbon pollution from new and existing power plants. Each state has a goal under this regulation. See your state's goal.

Transportation

Under the Clean Air Act, the Department of Transportation and EPA are making light-duty and heavy-duty vehicles more fuel efficient.

Landfills and Producers of Oil & Gas

Under the Clean Air Act, EPA is developing standards to address methane emissions from landfills and the oil and gas sector.

Industries Releasing Potent Greenhouse Gases

Under the Clean Air Act, EPA is moving to reduce the use and emissions of very potent greenhouse gases, HFCs, that are often used as refrigerants and in other industrial applications.

Commercial Buildings and Homes

Under the Energy Policy Act and the Energy Independence and Security Act, the U.S. Department of Energy is setting standards to make buildings more energy efficient.

1 The release of any gas that contributes to the greenhouse effect by absorbing infrared radiation, e.g., carbon dioxide and methane.

Power Plants

In the U.S., power plants are the single biggest source of greenhouse gas emissions. The U.S. is working with utilities to reduce greenhouse gases released by power plants in every state.
